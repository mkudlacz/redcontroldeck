>"...graffiti can make fascinating reading. They draw attention to themselves. They are an expression both of impotence and a kind of power-the power to disfigure..." D. Hebdige, Subculture, p. 3

With this comparison of subcultural practices to subway prophetics, Dick Hebdige concludes the introduction to his brief foray into the world of punk aesthetics, convinced that he alone, in the succeeding pages, can "tease out the meanings embedded in the various post-war youth styles (pg. 3) The faulty logic inherent in this proposition is obvious, but the project is further frustrated by his exclusion of the elements of regionalism and disregard for the evolutionary tendencies within style.' Within Hebdige's model, "punk" as a idea, punk style, the essence of the movement, i s a fixed place, a cultural code born in London in 1976, parent and superior to all other so-called movements. While this may be acceptable in the world of semiotics, from a historical perspective, it seems a bit short-sighted; these things cannot be excluded, lest the historian risks underestimating his subject group. 

The Lincoln punk scene, which blossomed between 1976 and 1984, is the perfect example. As an exception to Hebdige's rule, it perhaps best encapsulates the American, Midwestern punk experience. What Seven more, its message, its aspirations, are a record of the evolving modes of punk *style.*

During its heyday, 1976-1984, before the scene dissolved into hostile, dis-unified factions, it could be argued that, in fact, it was the fulfillment of punk as a platform – the fully evolved artifact in practice, a thing unknown to Hebdige, who failed to see past Sid Vicious and his radical promises of a world with no future. In fact, Nebraska punk, and Lincoln punk especially, can also be seen as a greater manifestation of cultural, social, and intellectual trends already present on a regional scale. This suggestion will of course, be met with some skepticism, but when examined within Elliot West's context of the West, bearing in mind his notions concerning the struggle for the western narrative, it seems quite possible.

The intent of this research then, is to discuss the punks in terms of their ties to these regional narratives, their coherence to and deviance from them, as well as the social conditions within the region at the time. Beyond this, of course, it is also the historian's duty to establish the rudimentary tenets of the Nebraska punk philosophy, and discuss the subculture in general, its evolving style and meaning, and its relations with the larger community which will not be neglected. Having amassed the goals of the discourse at hand, this position begs the question: to what end. Here, one can appeal to the historical tradition, and cross-apply the writings of other music historians who've concluded that: "...music is a direct form of expression. One can examine in depth various cultural groups through their music, their ways of life, value systems, aspirations and misery. Thus music is the key to understanding cultural history. " And, from
a more practical standpoint, music can, in Carney's estimation, "help create a sense of local awareness and, translated correctly, can become a source of state pride." Odd though it may seem, Nebraska punk has the potential to be such a rallying point.

##### Regionalism and the cultural tradition of resistance.
Early in Carney's discussion of the development of Oklahoma jazz, which serves as a nice point of departure for establishing the midwestern musical tradition, he refers to a quotation from Ralph Ellison which should not be dismissed.
>"The jazz artist who became nationally known is written about as though he came into existence only upon his arrival in New York. His career in big cities.... is stressed at the expense of his life in the South, the Southwest, and the Midwest, where most Negro musicians at least found their early development. Thus we are left with a n impression of mysterious rootlessness, and the true and often annoying complexity of American cultural experience is oversimplified."
Ellison's is indeed the same kind of frustration West finds in the "conflicting
narratives" of the Great Plains. The task at hand, therefore, is to illuminate
these narratives, to fight oversimplification, and to expose these roots. With the
story all told, it will then be clear that what is known as "punk," was, in Nebraska,
merely part of an even greater narrative of protest, revolt, and regionalism.

As early as the Populist era, midwesterners were part of the politics of protest, using music and performance as a means of voicing their concerns. In William Koch's 1983 essay on the protest songs of the Populist era, the seeds of punk rock seem already present. "Vast open air political gatherings," Koch writes, "were the order of the day and had dramatic impact if there was singing in addition to the speaking." Besides noting the participation of youngsters, Koch also highlights the highly emotional quality of the music as well as its rather proletariat message. For those with even the most elementaryunderstanding of punk dogma, the connection seems clear.
